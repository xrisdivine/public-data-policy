AI enables superhuman-scale aggregation, correlation, and exploitation — and the legal and policy frameworks haven't caught up.

The Problem

The Friction That Protected Us Is Gone

Government data is public by design — transparency, accountability, and civic participation depend on it. Court dockets, contract postings, licensing records, property filings. The default assumption has always been: public means accessible, and accessible is good.

But there's a gap between technically public and practically accessible. Historically, friction was the protection. You could go to the courthouse and look up every filing, but nobody was going to do that for millions of records. AI agents collapse that friction to zero.

Aggregation Risk

Individual records are innocuous. Combined across sources, they become surveillance. A court filing + a property record + a contract posting = a detailed profile nobody consented to.

Speed Asymmetry

Bad actors with AI pipelines act on public data faster than the people in that data can react. Predatory lenders targeting eviction filings the moment they hit the docket.

Scale Asymmetry

What was previously a targeted lookup becomes bulk harvesting. The intent shifts from "I need this specific record" to "I want every record matching this vulnerability pattern."

The technology to exploit this gap exists today. I know — because I built it. Not to cause harm, but the capabilities are identical to what a bad actor would build.

Two real products I built — one using public government data, the other using publicly available commercial data — that demonstrate how AI agents collapse the line between utility and exploitation. The data source differs. The risk pattern is identical.

Government Data — Federal Courts

DocketCast

Claude-powered litigation intelligence. Monitors new federal court filings daily via CourtListener, scores and summarizes cases using AI, and delivers personalized weekly digests to litigation support vendors.

The benign use

Helping expert witness firms and litigation support vendors find relevant, high-value cases efficiently. Saves hours of manual docket review every week.

The dark pattern

The same pipeline could profile litigants at scale. Combine eviction filings with medical debt cases and bankruptcy records to build vulnerability profiles. Front-run legal outcomes. Target people in active litigation with predatory services. Monitor specific individuals across every federal court in the country.

Policy Solutions

What Government Can Do Now

These aren't hypothetical. Each is technically feasible, has precedent, and preserves the transparency that makes public data valuable in the first place.

01

Query-Shaped Access, Not Download-Shaped Access

Redesign government data portals so the default interaction is asking questions, not downloading tables. Most legitimate uses are targeted: "show me this case," "what contracts posted this week." Exploitative uses are broad: "give me every eviction filing in this zip code for 5 years." Structure APIs to serve the first pattern freely and require justification for the second. This isn't access restriction — it's design that reflects actual use cases.

02

Differential Privacy for Aggregate Datasets

When bulk data must be published — spending reports, contract volumes, licensing stats — apply noise calibrated to individual sensitivity. The macro signal survives. The ability to re-identify or correlate individual records degrades. The Census Bureau proved this works at national scale. It's massively underdeployed everywhere else.

03

Purpose-Based Use Restrictions

Public data is currently governed by access law — FOIA, state sunshine laws. We need a complementary layer of use law. Access: still open. Use for individual lookup, journalism, research: permitted. Use for bulk profiling, predatory targeting, commercial resale without consent: prohibited. The EU's GDPR already partially does this. The US has no equivalent.

What I'd Build at Anthropic

A Concrete Action Plan

Five initiatives that Anthropic's Societal Impacts team is uniquely positioned to lead — combining technical capability, policy credibility, and the responsibility that comes from building the most capable AI systems in the world.

The Threat Library

Systematically red-team the top 20 most-accessed federal and state data sources — court dockets, property records, licensing databases, contract postings, campaign finance, business filings. For each one, document: "What's the most harmful pipeline an agent could build with this?" You can't mitigate what you haven't mapped.

The Anti-Aggregation Framework

The big policy contribution. Current law treats each dataset independently. The harm lives in the joins. Propose a legal standard that recognizes cross-source aggregation of public records as a distinct category requiring regulation. Not restricting access to any single source — creating liability when someone combines N sources to build profiles on individuals without consent.

The chemical precursor analogy: You can buy acetone. You can buy hydrogen peroxide. Each is legal. But combining them in certain quantities triggers regulation — not because any single ingredient is dangerous, but because the combination is. Public data aggregation needs the same principle.

Detection Built Into Claude

Not hard blocks — pattern detection. If an agent pipeline is scraping multiple government sources, correlating on individual identifiers, and targeting populations that map to vulnerability signals (debt, eviction, medical, criminal) — flag it for review. Same logic as fraud detection. You're looking for the pattern of exploitative aggregation, not restricting any individual query.

Responsible Scaling for Public Data

Similar to how Anthropic has ASL levels for catastrophic risk, create a framework specifically for public data exploitation. Define thresholds: at what capability level does an AI agent's ability to aggregate public records become a societal risk? What mitigations trigger at each level? Publish it. Commit to it. Challenge other labs to match it.

Government Partnerships

Help 2–3 pilot agencies redesign their data access patterns using the query-shaped model. Anthropic builds the threat model, the agency implements the fix, co-publish results. This creates credibility, demonstrates feasibility, and gives legislative staff a working example to point to.

The window to get this right is now — before the first major public-data exploitation incident forces reactive, poorly designed legislation that overcorrects and kills transparency altogether.
